Cats evolved as both predators and prey. As prey animals, hiding weakness and illness was a survival strategy — a sick cat who showed obvious signs of vulnerability in the wild was a cat that predators would target. That evolutionary instinct didn't disappear with domestication.

The result: your cat may be genuinely unwell for days or weeks before showing signs you'd recognize as "sick." By the time a cat is obviously suffering, they may have been ill for some time. Knowing the subtle early signs is one of the most important things you can do for your cat's health.

## 1. Changes in Eating or Drinking Habits

Cats are creatures of habit. A sudden change in appetite — eating significantly less, suddenly eating much more, or completely refusing food — is always worth attention.

**Loss of appetite (anorexia):** A cat who skips more than one meal is unusual. Two or more skipped meals warrants a vet call. Unlike dogs and humans, cats who don't eat for 48–72 hours can develop **hepatic lipidosis** (fatty liver disease), a serious and potentially fatal condition.

**Increased appetite:** Sudden ravenous hunger — especially in a middle-aged or older cat — can indicate hyperthyroidism or diabetes.

**Increased water consumption (polydipsia):** Drinking noticeably more water, or seeking out water from unusual sources (faucets, glasses), is a red flag for kidney disease, diabetes, or hyperthyroidism — all common in older cats.

## 2. Changes in Litter Box Habits

The litter box is an early warning system. Get in the habit of monitoring what's in there daily.

**Signs to watch:**
- Straining to urinate or producing only small amounts (may indicate UTI or blockage — a **urinary blockage in a male cat is a life-threatening emergency** requiring immediate vet care)
- Urinating outside the litter box (often a sign of pain, UTI, or stress)
- Blood in urine or stool
- Diarrhea lasting more than 48 hours
- Constipation — no stool for 48–72 hours
- Stool that is unusually loose, hard, or mucus-covered
- Defecating outside the litter box (particularly if this is new behavior)

## 3. Hiding More Than Usual

All cats hide sometimes. A cat who suddenly starts hiding consistently, coming out only to eat and use the litter box, or who stops engaging with people they normally seek out, is displaying a behavioral change that warrants attention.

Increased hiding is one of the most reliable early signs of illness, pain, or significant stress in cats. Trust this signal.

## 4. Changes in Grooming

**Over-grooming:** Compulsive licking that creates bald patches (usually on the belly, inner thighs, or flanks) can indicate allergies, parasites, pain in the area being licked, or psychogenic alopecia (stress-related overgrooming).

**Under-grooming:** A coat that looks dull, greasy, matted, or unkempt in a cat who used to be well-groomed indicates the cat can't or won't groom themselves normally. This can mean they're in pain (especially arthritis, which makes reaching certain areas difficult), unwell, or depressed. Older cats often stop grooming effectively due to dental pain, arthritis, or other illness.

## 5. Behavioral Changes and Personality Shifts

Know your cat's baseline. A cat who is normally social becoming withdrawn, or a typically reserved cat suddenly clingy, represents a departure from normal — and departures from normal are worth investigating.

**Watch for:**
- Increased irritability or aggression in a typically calm cat (pain makes cats defensive)
- Unusual vocalizations — yowling, especially at night (can indicate pain, cognitive dysfunction, or hyperthyroidism in older cats)
- Confusion, disorientation, or appearing to be "lost" in familiar territory (cognitive dysfunction, neurological issues)
- Decreased interest in play or interaction (can be early sign of many illnesses)

## 6. Changes in Breathing

Normal cats breathe silently and effortlessly. Any of the following warrant immediate veterinary attention:

- **Open-mouth breathing** — cats almost never breathe through their mouths except under extreme stress; if your cat is breathing open-mouthed at rest, this is an emergency
- **Labored breathing** — visibly working to breathe, belly moving heavily with each breath
- **Rapid breathing rate** (normal is 15–30 breaths per minute at rest)
- **Wheezing or coughing**
- Sitting in a "sphinx" position with elbows out (can indicate they're struggling to breathe)

Respiratory distress in cats is always an emergency.

## 7. Weight Loss

Weight loss in a cat is significant. Because cats are small animals, even 1–2 pounds represents a substantial percentage of their body weight. Unfortunately, it's easy to miss on a long-haired cat.

Run your hands along your cat's spine and ribs monthly. You should be able to feel (but not see) the ribs easily. If ribs are becoming prominent or the spine feels bony, your cat has lost body condition.

Weight loss in cats is associated with: hyperthyroidism, kidney disease, diabetes, cancer, dental disease, and many other conditions. It warrants a vet visit.

## 8. Eye and Nose Changes

**Eyes:**
- Third eyelid (haw) visible at the inner corner of the eye — indicates illness, pain, or parasites
- Discharge (especially yellow or green)
- Cloudiness, asymmetric pupils, or squinting
- Red or inflamed whites of the eyes

**Nose:**
- Any discharge (clear can be normal; yellow, green, or bloody is not)
- Persistent dry, cracked nose
- Sneezing fits lasting more than a day or two

## 9. Vocalization Changes

Cats communicate vocally primarily with humans, not with other cats. You know your cat's normal vocal repertoire. Changes are worth noting.

**Increased vocalization** — especially yowling or crying, particularly at night — is associated with hyperthyroidism, hypertension, pain, cognitive dysfunction (feline dementia), and hearing loss in older cats.

**Decreased vocalization** in a typically chatty cat can indicate depression, illness, or pain.

## 10. Mobility and Activity Changes

Watch for:
- **Reluctance to jump** that wasn't there before — arthritis is extremely common in cats over 10 and often goes undiagnosed because cats mask it
- **Stiffness after resting**, especially in cold weather
- **Changed gait** — limping, holding a leg, or unusual movement
- **Falling or loss of coordination**
- **Reduced activity or play** — a cat who used to zoom around at night lying flat instead

Arthritis in cats is vastly underdiagnosed. Most cats over 12 have arthritis, and many aren't receiving treatment because owners assume the reduced activity is "just getting old." Significant quality-of-life improvements are available with veterinary management.

## When to Call the Vet Immediately

Some signs need emergency care, not a wait-and-see approach:

- **Straining to urinate with nothing coming out** — especially in male cats — is an emergency
- **Open-mouth or labored breathing**
- **Seizures**
- **Suspected toxin ingestion**
- **Trauma** (fall from height, hit by car)
- **Collapse or loss of consciousness**
- **Pale, white, blue, or yellow gums** (jaundice, anemia, or shock)
- **Sudden hind leg weakness or paralysis** (aortic thromboembolism — "saddle thrombus" — is painful and urgent)
- **Complete food refusal for 48+ hours**

## The Annual Wellness Visit

Most of the conditions that cause early, subtle signs in cats — kidney disease, hyperthyroidism, diabetes, dental disease, hypertension — are detectable by bloodwork and physical exam before they cause obvious symptoms. Annual vet visits (twice yearly after age 10) are your first line of defense.

Cats age faster than their human owners tend to appreciate. A cat who was perfectly healthy at their annual exam can develop significant disease in 6–12 months. Don't skip the checkup because "they seem fine."

## The Bottom Line

Cats don't complain. They cope quietly, hide their weakness, and carry on until they can't. The most important thing you can do as a cat owner is know your individual cat's normal — their baseline appetite, activity, litter box habits, and personality — so you notice when something shifts.

When in doubt, call your vet. A quick phone consultation is almost always free and will tell you whether what you're seeing warrants concern. Finding problems early almost always means better outcomes and lower treatment costs.
