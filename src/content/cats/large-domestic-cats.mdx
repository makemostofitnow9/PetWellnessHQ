If you've ever wanted a cat that feels more like a small dog — one that follows you room to room, greets you at the door, and takes up half the couch — a large domestic cat breed might be exactly what you're looking for.

Large cats aren't just novelty. They tend to be calm, sociable, and well-suited to family life. The tradeoff: more food, a bigger litter box, and in many cases, a significant grooming commitment.

Here are the 12 largest domestic cat breeds, ranked with everything you need to know before bringing one home.

### 1. Maine Coon

The Maine Coon is the undisputed giant of the domestic cat world. Males routinely reach 15–20 pounds, and some exceptional individuals have been recorded at over 25 pounds. They have long, muscular bodies, tufted ears, and a thick, shaggy coat designed for cold New England winters.

Despite their size, Maine Coons have a gentle, almost dog-like personality. They're talkative — not with a loud meow but with a characteristic chirping or trilling sound. They love water, will fetch toys, and follow their owners from room to room. They're excellent with children and other pets.

Grooming requirement is moderate for their coat length. Weekly brushing prevents mats, but the coat is silky enough that it doesn't tangle as easily as a Persian's. Maine Coons are generally healthy, though HCM (hypertrophic cardiomyopathy) screening for breeding cats is important.

### 2. Ragdoll

Ragdolls earn their name from their habit of going completely limp when picked up — a trait that makes them extraordinarily easy to handle. They're large, with males reaching 15–20 pounds, but they mature slowly and don't reach full size until age 4.

Their coat is semi-long, silky, and comes in a limited palette of pointed colors (seal, blue, chocolate, lilac, and more). Unlike many longhaired cats, Ragdolls shed minimally and are considered easier to groom than their coat suggests.

Personality-wise, Ragdolls are calm, affectionate, and tolerant to a fault. They're not highly active cats — they're content to sit on the couch with you for hours. This makes them ideal for quieter households or families with young children who want a gentle giant.

If you're considering a kitten, read our [Ragdoll kitten care guide](/cats/ragdoll-kitten) for detailed information on development, feeding, and finding a reputable breeder.

### 3. Norwegian Forest Cat

The Norwegian Forest Cat — called "Wegie" by fans — looks like a fairy-tale cat. They have a thick double coat, a bushy tail, and a robust, muscular body built for climbing and hunting in Scandinavian forests. Males weigh 9–16 pounds.

Wegies are friendly and sociable but less clingy than Ragdolls or Maine Coons. They're independent thinkers who enjoy human company on their own terms. They're athletic and love to climb — a tall cat tree is nearly mandatory. They handle cold weather extremely well and are sometimes found in multi-cat households where they establish a calm hierarchy.

Their coat sheds significantly in spring and requires weekly brushing year-round, with more frequent grooming during shedding season.

### 4. Siberian

The Siberian is Russia's native forest cat — a large, powerful breed with a triple-layer coat that evolved to survive brutal winters. Males typically weigh 12–17 pounds. What makes the Siberian particularly interesting is its reputation as a lower-allergen option: they produce less Fel d 1 protein (the main cat allergen) than most breeds, though no cat is truly hypoallergenic.

Siberians are playful, affectionate, and surprisingly agile for their size. They love interactive play and tend to remain kitten-like well into adulthood. They get along well with children and dogs. Their coat, despite its volume, resists matting and requires brushing two to three times per week.

### 5. Savannah

The Savannah is a hybrid breed — a cross between a domestic cat and a serval (a wild African cat). The result is the tallest domestic cat breed, with some individuals standing 17–20 inches at the shoulder. Weight varies by generation (F1 through F5): F1 Savannahs (closest to the serval) can exceed 20 pounds and have the most wild traits; F5 and beyond are much more domesticated.

Savannahs are not for first-time cat owners. They're highly active, extremely intelligent, and can open doors, drawers, and cabinets. They need significant vertical space, daily interactive play, and mental stimulation. In the right home — active owners, lots of space — they're remarkable companions. Check your local laws before purchasing, as some states restrict serval hybrids.

### 6. British Shorthair

The British Shorthair is the quintessential stocky, dignified cat. Males weigh 9–17 pounds, with a round face, dense plush coat, and copper or gold eyes that give them a permanently wise expression. They're not a slender cat — their build is solid and compact.

British Shorthairs are calm, quiet, and fairly independent. They're affectionate without being needy and adapt well to apartment living. They're not lap cats by nature, but they enjoy being near their people. Grooming is minimal — their short, dense coat needs weekly brushing.

Health note: British Shorthairs can be prone to HCM and PKD (polycystic kidney disease). Reputable breeders test for both.

### 7. Turkish Van

The Turkish Van is a large, semi-longhaired cat with a distinctive coat pattern: white body with color only on the head and tail (called the "Van pattern"). Males reach 9–20 pounds. They're known for an unusual affinity for water — some will voluntarily swim.

Turkish Vans are active, intelligent, and often described as dog-like. They bond strongly with their families but can be selective about who they interact with. They're not ideal for sedentary owners — they need play, stimulation, and space to move.

### 8. Chartreux

The Chartreux is a French breed with a distinctive blue-grey coat and orange eyes. Males weigh 10–16 pounds. Their coat has a woolly texture unlike any other breed — it parts like a sheep's fleece when touched.

Chartreux are quiet cats — some are nearly silent despite being communicative through body language and expression. They're loyal, intelligent, and remarkably adaptable. They bond closely with one person but are friendly to the whole family. They're good mousers with lightning-fast reflexes despite their stocky build.

### 9. Birman

The Birman (not to be confused with the Burmese) is a silky-coated, colorpointed cat with the breed's signature white "gloves" on all four paws. Males reach 7–14 pounds — medium-large, not a true giant, but substantial.

Birmans are gentle, affectionate, and quiet. They're good with children and other pets, and they love to be involved in household activity without being disruptive. Their silky coat is surprisingly low-maintenance for its length — it doesn't mat as easily as a Persian's because it lacks a dense undercoat.

### 10. Scottish Fold

Scottish Folds are recognizable immediately by their folded ears, which give them an owl-like appearance. Males weigh 7–13 pounds. They have round faces, round eyes, and a round, compact body.

Scottish Folds are calm, adaptable, and tend to sit in odd positions — flat on their backs, standing upright on their hind legs — that owners find endlessly entertaining. They're affectionate without being demanding. 

Important health note: All Scottish Folds carry a genetic mutation (the fold gene) linked to a painful cartilage condition called osteochondrodysplasia. Responsible breeding practices are essential; always buy from a breeder who health-tests their cats.

### 11. Chausie

The Chausie is a hybrid between domestic cats and the jungle cat (Felis chaus). They're tall, long, and athletic — males can reach 9–15 pounds — with a wild appearance but a domestic temperament (particularly in later generations).

Chausies are highly active, intelligent, and need significant daily interaction and play. They're not well-suited to being left alone for long periods. In the right home with an active owner and plenty of enrichment, they're remarkable, loyal companions.

### 12. Bengal

The Bengal is the most popular exotic-looking domestic cat breed — a cross between domestic cats and Asian leopard cats. Males weigh 10–15 pounds and have a striking spotted or marbled coat that looks like a miniature leopard.

Bengals are highly intelligent, energetic, and curious. They need interactive play every day. They enjoy water (some will play in a running faucet), are excellent climbers, and can become destructive if under-stimulated. They're best suited to active owners who can provide daily engagement. For experienced cat owners willing to invest the time, they're endlessly fascinating.

## What to Consider Before Getting a Large Cat

**Space:** Large cats need room to move. A small apartment isn't disqualifying, but you'll want vertical space — tall cat trees, wall-mounted shelves, and window perches.

**Food costs:** A 15-pound cat eats roughly twice what a 7-pound cat eats. Factor in $60–$100/month for quality food.

**Vet costs:** Large cats aren't inherently more expensive to treat, but some large breeds (Maine Coon, Ragdoll, British Shorthair) have elevated risks of heart disease (HCM). Pet insurance is worth considering.

**Grooming:** Most large cat breeds are longhaired or semi-longhaired. Budget time for regular brushing — especially during spring shedding season.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is the largest domestic cat breed?
The Maine Coon holds the record for the longest domestic cat and is consistently among the heaviest, with males averaging 13–18 pounds. The Savannah can be taller at the shoulder but is lighter in build.

### Are large cat breeds good with kids?
Generally yes — Ragdolls, Maine Coons, Birmans, and British Shorthairs are all known for their gentle, tolerant temperaments with children. High-energy hybrids like Savannahs and Bengals are better suited to older children who understand boundaries.

### Do large cats live as long as smaller cats?
Large cat breeds tend to have similar lifespans to average-sized cats — 12–15 years for most breeds. Maine Coons and Ragdolls commonly live 12–16 years with good care.

### Are large cats more expensive?
Yes, in several ways. Kittens from reputable breeders cost $1,000–$3,000+ for breeds like Ragdoll, Maine Coon, and Savannah. Ongoing food and vet costs are also higher due to their size.

### Can large cats live in apartments?
Most large cat breeds can adapt to apartment living, provided they have enough vertical space (tall cat trees) and daily interactive play. Savannah cats and Chausies are the exceptions — they need more room to move.
