"Pitbull" is one of the most misused words in the dog world. It's applied loosely to dozens of dogs, used inconsistently by animal shelters, media outlets, and legislators, and means something different depending on who you ask. In reality, the term "pitbull" describes not a single breed but a loosely related group of dogs that share common ancestry in 19th-century bull-and-terrier type dogs originally bred in England.

There are five breeds that are most commonly recognized under the pitbull umbrella: the American Pit Bull Terrier, the American Staffordshire Terrier, the Staffordshire Bull Terrier, the American Bully, and the American Bulldog. Each has a distinct history, official breed standard, and temperament profile. Understanding the differences matters — both for prospective owners making an informed decision and for anyone interested in cutting through the noise that surrounds this group of dogs.

This article covers each of the five types in detail: their origins, physical characteristics, temperament, and what life with each breed actually looks like.

## The 5 Types of Pitbulls

### 1. American Pit Bull Terrier (APBT)

The American Pit Bull Terrier is the breed most closely associated with the "pitbull" label and the one from which the colloquial term derives. The APBT traces its origins to 19th-century England, where bulldogs were crossed with terriers to create dogs that combined the bulldog's strength and tenacity with the terrier's agility and gameness. These dogs were initially used in blood sports — bull-baiting and then dogfighting — practices that, while illegal today and deeply inhumane, shaped the breed's physical and temperamental characteristics.

When these dogs were brought to America with Irish and English immigrants in the mid-1800s, they were used as all-purpose farm dogs — hunting, herding, guarding property, and serving as family companions. "Stubby," one of the most decorated military dogs in American history, was an APBT mix. They appeared in early American advertising as symbols of patriotism and were regularly referred to as "America's dog" in popular culture through the early 20th century.

The APBT is recognized by the United Kennel Club (UKC) but not by the American Kennel Club (AKC), which recognizes the closely related American Staffordshire Terrier instead. APBTs are medium-sized dogs, typically weighing 30–65 pounds, with a lean, muscular build, a broad head, and a short, low-maintenance coat. They come in virtually every color and pattern.

Temperamentally, well-bred American Pit Bull Terriers are characteristically people-oriented — enthusiastic, affectionate, and often described as "love bugs" by owners. They tend to be social, outgoing, and eager for human attention. The UKC breed standard explicitly states that human aggression is a serious fault, and APBTs were historically bred to be handled by people even under stressful conditions. Dog-to-dog aggression, however, can be a concern, stemming from their fighting heritage, and socialization, management, and training should address this.

### 2. American Staffordshire Terrier (AmStaff)

The American Staffordshire Terrier shares common ancestry with the APBT — both descend from the same 19th-century bull-and-terrier crosses. The divergence between the two breeds occurred primarily because of AKC recognition: when breeders sought AKC recognition in the 1930s, the APBT was renamed the American Staffordshire Terrier for the purpose of registration, and the two lines developed separately from that point.

Today, an AmStaff and an APBT may look very similar — or quite different — depending on the breeder and the generations of selection involved. AmStaffs are generally slightly larger and more heavily built than the leaner sport-lines of APBT, reflecting generations of breeding toward the AKC conformation standard rather than athletic performance. They typically weigh 40–70 pounds and have a broader chest, heavier bone, and a stockier build.

Temperamentally, the AmStaff is remarkably similar to the APBT: people-loving, energetic, confident, and affectionate. They tend to be highly trainable and excel in obedience, agility, and nose work. They're often used as therapy dogs, a role that showcases their gentle, emotionally intuitive nature. Like the APBT, they may show dog selectivity, and responsible ownership includes proper socialization and management.

AmStaffs are currently recognized by the AKC and are part of the Terrier Group. They're a popular choice for families who want the classic "pitbull" look and temperament in a dog that's recognizable within AKC registration systems — which can matter in housing and insurance contexts.

### 3. Staffordshire Bull Terrier

The Staffordshire Bull Terrier — affectionately known as the "Staffie" — is the original English version of the pitbull-type dog. Developed in the Staffordshire region of England in the early 19th century for the same bull-and-terrier blood sports, the Staffie was eventually refined after the sports were banned into the devoted, sweet-natured family companion it is today. It was recognized by the Kennel Club in England in 1935.

Staffies are significantly smaller than their American counterparts, typically weighing just 24–38 pounds. Despite their compact size, they're powerfully built with broad heads, well-sprung ribs, and muscular hindquarters. Their coat is short and smooth. They come in red, fawn, white, black, blue, or brindle, with or without white markings.

The Staffordshire Bull Terrier has a well-earned reputation in the UK as a devoted family dog and is particularly noted for its affinity with children — they're sometimes called the "nanny dog" in British culture, though that specific nickname should not be taken as license to leave any dog unsupervised with children. Their size makes them more manageable than the larger American types, and their temperament tends to be somewhat less intense. Staffies are exuberant, playful, and loving to an almost overwhelming degree.

Staffies are recognized by both the AKC and the UKC and are generally considered the most universally family-friendly of the pitbull-type breeds. They still benefit from training and socialization, particularly for dog-to-dog interactions, but they're often a good starting point for first-time bully breed owners.

### 4. American Bully

The American Bully is the newest of the five types, developed in the United States in the 1980s and 1990s by breeders who wanted a companion-focused dog with the iconic "bully" look — broad chest, heavy bone, wide head — but with a calmer, less drive-intensive temperament than the APBT or AmStaff. The American Bully was created by crossing APBTs with American Bulldogs, English Bulldogs, and various other bulldog-type breeds.

The result is a breed that is distinctly different from the APBT in temperament despite sharing some physical similarities. The American Bully is less intense, less driven, and lower-energy than the traditional pit bull terrier types. They were bred specifically as companion dogs, and this purpose is evident in their disposition: they tend to be extraordinarily affectionate, patient, and family-oriented.

The American Bully comes in four recognized size classes: Pocket (under 17 inches), Standard (17–20 inches), Classic (similar build to the Standard), and XL (20–23 inches). XL and XXL "American Bullies" can weigh 80–150 pounds or more, and the extreme size variants are the product of selective breeding that prioritizes mass and build. These extreme variants are not recognized by the American Bully Kennel Club (ABKC).

American Bullies were recognized by the United Kennel Club in 2013. Their temperament standard emphasizes gentleness, confidence, and affection — they're expected to be friendly with strangers, children, and other animals. In practice, well-bred American Bullies often live up to this standard. They're one of the most family-friendly bully breeds, with a loyal but relaxed disposition well-suited to home life.

### 5. American Bulldog

The American Bulldog is the most physically distinct of the five types and arguably has the weakest historical connection to the traditional "pitbull" dogs, though it's frequently included in the category due to its broad-headed, muscular appearance. American Bulldogs descend from the original working bulldogs brought to America by English settlers in the 17th and 18th centuries — similar ancestors to the APBT, but the two lines diverged centuries ago.

American Bulldogs were used in the American South as all-purpose farm dogs: catching feral hogs and cattle, protecting property, and hunting. They're larger than the other pitbull-type breeds, typically weighing 60–120 pounds, with a robust, athletic build. There are two recognized subtypes: the Classic (Johnson) type, which is heavier and more bulldog-like, and the Performance (Scott) type, which is leaner and more athletic.

Temperamentally, American Bulldogs are confident, loyal, and assertive. They're devoted to their families and can be excellent companions, but they tend to be more reserved with strangers than the other breeds on this list. They were bred as working guardians, and that protective instinct is still present. Early socialization is critically important to ensure appropriate behavior in a variety of situations. They can show dog aggression, particularly toward same-sex dogs, and should be managed accordingly.

American Bulldogs are athletic and need regular, vigorous exercise. They're not typically content to be couch potatoes — they need a job or significant daily activity to stay mentally and physically healthy. They're recognized by the United Kennel Club and are considered a working breed.

## Common Myths About Pitbulls

Few groups of dogs are as consistently misrepresented as pitbull-type breeds. Let's address the most pervasive myths with what the evidence actually shows.

**Myth: Pitbulls have locking jaws.**
This is anatomically false. No dog breed has a jaw that "locks" — there's no unique jaw mechanism in any pitbull type. The myth persists partly because bully breeds are physically strong and can bite with significant force, but their jaw structure is no different from other dogs of comparable size.

**Myth: Pitbulls are inherently dangerous and unpredictable.**
Decades of behavioral research and temperament testing do not support this claim. The American Temperament Test Society (ATTS) tests dogs for stability, aggressiveness, friendliness, and protectiveness — APBTs and AmStaffs consistently pass at rates of 87–88%, higher than many popular breeds like the Chihuahua, Lhasa Apso, and Cocker Spaniel. Aggression in dogs is influenced by individual genetics, socialization, training, and history — not breed alone.

**Myth: Once a pitbull bites, it won't stop.**
There is no credible behavioral or physiological basis for this claim. Individual bite inhibition varies by dog and training history, not by breed type. Any dog that has been trained for aggression or has a history of abuse may have compromised bite inhibition, but this is true regardless of breed.

**Myth: You can't train a pitbull.**
Pitbull-type breeds are actually among the most trainable dogs, particularly with positive reinforcement methods. Their eagerness to please their owners (particularly the APBT and AmStaff), coupled with their high intelligence and energy, makes them excellent candidates for obedience, agility, therapy work, and service dog roles.

**Myth: Pitbulls should never be trusted around children.**
The opposite is historically closer to the truth — many pitbull-type dogs were famously used as family dogs and companions for children. "Petey" from The Little Rascals was an APBT. That said, as with any dog, supervision and teaching children how to interact appropriately with dogs is always important.

## A Note on Breed-Specific Legislation (BSL)

Breed-specific legislation refers to laws that restrict or ban specific dog breeds, typically targeting pitbull-type dogs. BSL exists at the municipal, county, and national level in various locations around the world — some jurisdictions ban pitbull-type dogs outright, while others require owners to carry specific liability insurance, muzzle dogs in public, or obtain special permits.

Before bringing a pitbull-type dog into your home, it is essential to research the laws in your specific city, county, and municipality. These laws can change, and they're enforced at the local level — which means your state may permit the breed while your city does not. Housing regulations and homeowner's or renter's insurance policies may also restrict certain breeds regardless of local law.

The major animal welfare organizations — including the American Veterinary Medical Association, the American Kennel Club, and the National Canine Research Council — oppose breed-specific legislation, citing evidence that it is ineffective at reducing dog bites and imposes significant hardship on responsible owners without addressing the root causes of dangerous dog behavior. However, understanding the legal landscape where you live is a practical necessity for prospective pitbull owners.

## FAQ: Types of Pitbulls

### Are pitbulls dangerous dogs?

The short answer is no more so than many other breeds, and less so than popular perception suggests. Research consistently shows that dog bite risk is determined by individual factors — socialization, training, history of abuse, health status — rather than breed. Pitbull-type dogs are strong animals, which means any behavioral issue they develop has greater potential for harm than the same issue in a smaller dog. This is why training, socialization, and responsible ownership are especially important for these breeds — but the same is true of any large, powerful dog.

### Are pitbulls good family dogs?

Yes, with proper training and socialization, most pitbull-type breeds are excellent family dogs. The APBT, AmStaff, and Staffordshire Bull Terrier are particularly noted for their affection and patience with family members, including children. The American Bully was specifically bred to maximize these family-friendly qualities. As with all dogs, children should always be supervised and taught to interact respectfully with the dog.

### What's the difference between an American Pit Bull Terrier and an American Staffordshire Terrier?

The two breeds share common ancestry and are physically similar, but they've been developed as separate breeds since the 1930s. The main practical differences are that the APBT is recognized by the UKC (not the AKC), tends toward a leaner, more athletic build in sport lines, and was developed with more emphasis on physical performance. The AmStaff is recognized by the AKC and has been bred more toward conformation standards, resulting in a generally heavier, broader build.

### How do I train a pitbull?

Positive reinforcement — rewarding desired behavior with treats, praise, or play — is the most effective and humane training approach for pitbull-type dogs. They're highly motivated by food and human approval, which makes them very responsive to reward-based training. Training should begin early (as soon as you bring the puppy home), be consistent, and include socialization to a wide variety of people, animals, environments, and situations. Classes with a certified professional trainer are strongly recommended, particularly for first-time bully breed owners.

### Do pitbulls need a lot of exercise?

The APBT, AmStaff, Staffordshire Bull Terrier, and American Bulldog are all energetic dogs that need daily vigorous exercise — at minimum, 45–60 minutes of active physical activity per day. Mental stimulation through training, puzzle feeders, and interactive play is equally important. The American Bully tends to be calmer and has lower exercise requirements, though they still benefit from daily walks and play sessions. A well-exercised pitbull is a calm, well-behaved housemate; an under-exercised one can become frustrated, destructive, or difficult to manage.

### Are pitbulls good with other dogs?

This varies significantly by individual dog, breed type, and history. As a general rule, pitbull-type breeds were selectively bred for dog-to-dog combat over many generations, and some individuals retain elevated dog-to-dog aggression. This doesn't mean all pitbulls are dog-aggressive — many live peacefully with other dogs — but it does mean that socialization from an early age, careful introductions to new dogs, and ongoing management are important. Opposite-sex pairings tend to have fewer compatibility issues than same-sex pairings. An assessment by a certified behaviorist before adding a second dog to a pitbull household is a wise step.
