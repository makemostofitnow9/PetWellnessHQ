There's something about a truly fluffy dog that's almost impossible to resist. Whether it's the cloud-white coat of a Samoyed, the lion-like mane of a Leonberger, or the tiny puffball silhouette of a Pomeranian, fluffy dogs have a way of stopping people in their tracks. They look like living stuffed animals — warm, inviting, and endlessly squeezable.

But owning a fluffy dog is not for the unprepared. Most of the breeds on this list carry dense, double-layered coats that require serious grooming commitment. They shed — sometimes dramatically — and without regular brushing, that gorgeous fluff can turn into a matted, uncomfortable mess. The good news is that for owners willing to put in the time (or the money for a good groomer), a fluffy dog is one of the most rewarding pets you can have.

This guide covers 20 of the most beautiful, cuddly, and famously fluffy dog breeds in the world, from lap-sized puffballs to gentle giants with coats that could rival a polar bear's.

## The 20 Fluffiest Dog Breeds

### 1. Samoyed

If any dog could be described as a living cloud, it's the Samoyed. Originating from Siberia, where the Samoyedic peoples used them for herding reindeer and pulling sleds in brutal Arctic conditions, the Samoyed developed one of the most spectacular coats in the canine world. Their thick, white double coat stands away from the body in a plush halo, and their permanently upturned corners of the mouth give them the famous "Sammy smile."

Samoyeds are friendly, social, and surprisingly vocal — they communicate with their owners through a range of barks, howls, and singing-like vocalizations. They're gentle with children and generally good with other animals. They do need significant daily exercise and mental engagement, as bored Samoyeds can become destructive. They're not the best choice for hot climates, as their coat provides insulation in both directions.

Grooming a Samoyed is a serious commitment. Their coat sheds heavily twice a year ("blowing coat"), and during these periods daily brushing for 30–60 minutes is not an exaggeration. Even outside shedding season, regular brushing several times per week is essential. The good news: Samoyed hair is famously hypoallergenic (relatively speaking) and some crafty owners spin it into yarn.

### 2. Chow Chow

The Chow Chow is one of the oldest dog breeds in the world, with a history stretching back over 2,000 years to ancient China, where they were used for hunting, herding, and guarding. Their most striking feature — besides that famous blue-black tongue — is their incredibly dense, lion-like mane around the neck and shoulders. Chow Chows come in both rough-coated and smooth-coated varieties, with the rough coat being the more dramatically fluffy of the two.

Chow Chows are famously independent and cat-like in their personality. They're fiercely loyal to their immediate family but tend to be aloof with strangers. They're not typically lap dogs — they're dignified, serious animals who prefer respect over excessive affection. They require experienced owners who understand calm, consistent leadership. Early and ongoing socialization is critical for this breed.

Grooming the rough-coated Chow requires brushing several times per week to prevent mat formation, particularly in the mane and hindquarters. They shed heavily during seasonal coat changes. Their skin folds around the face need regular cleaning to prevent infections. Despite the high grooming demands, Chow Chows are relatively clean dogs who tend to be easy to housebreak.

### 3. Pomeranian

The Pomeranian is proof that the biggest personality doesn't always come in the biggest package. Weighing just 3–7 pounds, the Pom carries a luxuriously thick double coat that fans out in a glorious puffball, complete with a thick ruff around the neck and a heavily plumed tail curled over the back. They look like a fox-faced teddy bear, and it's nearly impossible to walk one without attracting a crowd.

Pomeranians are bold, lively, and confident to an almost comical degree. They have no concept of their own small size and will confront much larger dogs without hesitation. They're highly trainable and excel in obedience and agility, often surprising people with their athleticism. They can be vocal, so teaching a "quiet" command early is recommended. They're excellent apartment dogs as long as they get daily walks and mental stimulation.

Their coat requires brushing 2–3 times per week to prevent matting, with more frequent brushing during the twice-yearly shedding season. Professional grooming every 1–2 months is recommended to maintain the shape of the coat. Poms can experience "black skin disease" (alopecia X), a condition that causes hair loss — a reputable breeder will screen for this.

### 4. Bichon Frise

The Bichon Frise's name translates from French as "curly lap dog," and the name is fitting on every count. Their coat is a dense, white cloud of small curls that gives them a perfectly round, powder-puff silhouette. Unlike many fluffy breeds, the Bichon is low-shedding — their curly coat traps loose hairs internally rather than releasing them into the environment, making them a good option for allergy sufferers who still want plenty of fluff.

Bichons are joyful, playful, and perpetually cheerful. They get along well with virtually everyone — children, strangers, other pets — and their gentle nature makes them outstanding therapy dogs. They thrive on human interaction and can develop separation anxiety if left alone for extended periods. They adapt well to apartment living as long as they get daily exercise and regular attention.

The Bichon's coat requires daily brushing to prevent mats and tangles, and professional grooming every 4–6 weeks is standard. The round "powder puff" trim requires skilled scissor work to achieve and maintain. Tear staining is a common concern, and keeping the area around their eyes clean is an ongoing maintenance task.

### 5. Bernese Mountain Dog

The Bernese Mountain Dog is a Swiss working breed with a striking tri-colored coat — jet black with white and rust markings — and a body built for mountain terrain. Their long, slightly wavy outer coat sits over a dense undercoat, creating the lush, soft texture that makes them one of the most beloved large fluffy breeds. Berners are impressive animals that somehow manage to look both powerful and cuddly simultaneously.

Berners are calm, gentle, and devoted family dogs. They're patient with children, good-natured with strangers, and generally easygoing with other animals. They thrive in cooler climates and love outdoor activities — hiking, cart-pulling, and just exploring the backyard. They can be relatively low-energy indoors but need regular outdoor exercise to stay healthy and content. One important note: Bernese Mountain Dogs have a shorter-than-average lifespan, typically 7–10 years, and are prone to several health conditions including cancer and hip dysplasia.

Their coat sheds moderately year-round and heavily twice annually. Brushing 2–3 times per week (daily during shedding season) significantly reduces hair accumulation in the home. Professional grooming every few months helps manage the volume of coat. A quality vacuum cleaner is an essential household item for Berner owners.

### 6. Great Pyrenees

The Great Pyrenees is a majestic livestock guardian dog from the mountains between France and Spain, developed to protect sheep from wolves and bears in high-altitude terrain. Their thick, weather-resistant double coat is primarily white, sometimes with gray, tan, or reddish-brown markings, and provides insulation against extreme cold. In full coat, they look like a small polar bear — which is perhaps the highest compliment in the fluffy dog world.

Pyrenees are independent, patient, and calm — qualities essential for a dog that must make autonomous decisions while guarding a flock on a mountainside. They're gentle and affectionate with their family but naturally wary of strangers, making them effective watchdogs. They tend to bark — especially at night — and are known for their nocturnal patrolling behavior. A securely fenced yard is essential, as they're prone to roaming.

Their coat is surprisingly easy to maintain given its volume — the texture naturally resists matting and tends to shed dirt rather than hold it. Weekly brushing is generally sufficient outside shedding season, though the heavy twice-yearly shed requires much more frequent attention. They do not do well in hot, humid climates and should be kept cool in summer.

### 7. Keeshond

The Keeshond is the national dog of the Netherlands, originally bred as a companion and guard dog on Dutch riverboats and barges. They have a spectacular silver-and-black double coat that stands off the body in a luxurious mane, with distinctive "spectacles" — dark markings and shadings around the eyes that give them an alert, wise expression. The Keeshond is among the most visually striking of all medium-sized fluffy dogs.

Keeshonden (the proper plural) are friendly, intelligent, and exceptionally people-oriented. They're known as "Velcro dogs" — always at their owner's side, always wanting to be involved in whatever is happening. They're good with children and other pets, adapt well to various living situations, and are relatively easy to train. They can be vocal, as their heritage as watchdogs predisposes them to alert barking.

Their double coat requires brushing twice a week during normal periods, increasing to daily during shedding season. Bathing should be done carefully, ensuring the coat is thoroughly dried — the dense undercoat can stay damp for a long time and lead to skin issues if not dried properly. Professional grooming a few times a year helps keep the coat in optimal condition.

### 8. American Eskimo Dog

Despite the name, the American Eskimo Dog is actually descended from German Spitz breeds brought to the United States by German immigrants in the 19th century. They come in three sizes — Toy (6–10 lbs), Miniature (10–20 lbs), and Standard (25–35 lbs) — all with the same beautiful white double coat, erect ears, and alert, fox-like face. Their coat has a natural, sparkling white appearance that requires surprisingly little work to maintain.

American Eskimo Dogs are intelligent, trainable, and eager to please — qualities that made them popular circus dogs in the early 20th century. They're energetic and playful, excellent with children, and thrive in active households. They can be reserved with strangers initially but warm up quickly. They require daily exercise and significant mental stimulation; a bored Eskie can become destructive or develop obsessive behaviors.

Their coats are dense but relatively easy to maintain. The white coat naturally repels dirt and has a Teflon-like quality that keeps it looking clean longer than you'd expect. Brushing twice a week is standard, with daily brushing during heavy shedding periods. They don't typically require professional grooming, though a bath and blow-dry every month or two helps manage the coat.

### 9. Old English Sheepdog

The Old English Sheepdog — famously associated with the Dulux paint brand in the UK — is one of the most recognizable fluffy large dog breeds. Their shaggy, abundant double coat covers them from nose to tail, often obscuring their eyes beneath a thick fringe of hair. Their coat can be any shade of gray or blue with white markings, and the texture is both profuse and adaptable to different weather conditions.

Old English Sheepdogs are gentle, adaptable, and clownish. They're known for their sense of humor and their love of play, and they tend to retain a puppy-like playfulness well into adulthood. They're patient with children and generally good-natured with other animals. They were bred as drover dogs — herding cattle and sheep to market — and retain intelligence and trainability from that background.

Their coat is extraordinarily high-maintenance. Without daily brushing, it mats aggressively throughout — particularly in problem areas like behind the ears, under the "armpits," and around the hindquarters. Many owners choose to keep the coat clipped short for practicality. Professional grooming is almost always necessary. This is not a breed for owners unwilling to commit serious time and money to coat maintenance.

### 10. Newfoundland

The Newfoundland is a gentle giant with a heart as massive as its body. Originally bred as a working dog for fishermen on the coast of Newfoundland, Canada, these dogs were used for hauling nets, retrieving objects from icy water, and even rescuing drowning sailors. Their dense, water-resistant double coat — usually black, brown, or landseer (black and white) — provides protection in cold, wet conditions.

Newfoundlands are famously sweet-natured, patient, and nurturing — they're often called "nanny dogs" for their gentle, protective behavior around children. They're calm indoors and devoted to their families. Despite their size, they're not overly energetic; they're steady and measured rather than boisterous. They do need regular exercise and cool environments, as they're prone to overheating.

Their coat sheds significantly year-round and requires brushing at least 2–3 times per week to manage. They also drool considerably, and that drool finds its way into the coat, requiring regular cleaning. Bathing a Newfoundland is a logistical endeavor given their size, and professional grooming is often the practical choice. The fur-and-drool combination is something potential owners should seriously consider.


### 11. Shetland Sheepdog

The Shetland Sheepdog — or Sheltie — is a miniature version of the rough-coated Collie (think Lassie), developed on the windswept Shetland Islands off the coast of Scotland. Their coat is a glorious double layer with a long, straight outer coat and a dense, woolly undercoat, framing their narrow, elegant face with a sweeping mane. They come in sable, black, and blue merle, often with white and tan markings.

Shelties are brilliant, sensitive, and extraordinarily trainable. They consistently rank among the top 10 most intelligent dog breeds and excel in obedience, agility, and herding competitions. They're loyal and affectionate with their families but can be reserved or shy with strangers. They tend to be vocal — alert barking is built into their heritage as farm watchdogs — and they have strong herding instincts that sometimes manifest as chasing or circling family members.

Their double coat requires brushing at least 2–3 times per week to prevent mats, with special attention to the areas behind the ears and on the legs. They shed heavily twice a year, and daily brushing during these periods is not excessive. Professional grooming a few times annually helps with de-shedding and trimming of sanitary areas.

### 12. Eurasier

The Eurasier is a relatively new breed developed in Germany in the 1960s by crossing the Chow Chow, Wolfspitz (Keeshond), and later the Samoyed. The result is a medium-sized spitz-type dog with a thick, plush double coat that comes in a wide range of colors. They're still rare outside of Europe but have a devoted following among those who've discovered them.

Eurasiers are calm, even-tempered, and deeply devoted to their family — but on their own terms. They're not overly demonstrative and tend to be reserved with strangers, but they form profound bonds with their household members. They're intelligent and trainable, though they can be independent. They need a family that will genuinely include them in daily life; they do not thrive in kennels or with owners who are frequently away.

Their coat requires brushing 2–3 times per week normally, increasing during twice-yearly shedding periods. The coat is relatively self-cleaning and requires infrequent bathing. Eurasiers are generally healthy dogs with fewer breed-specific concerns than some of their parent breeds, which is one of their less-publicized advantages.

### 13. Tibetan Mastiff

The Tibetan Mastiff is an ancient breed from the Himalayan mountains, used for centuries to guard Buddhist monasteries and nomadic campsites. They're one of the largest and most visually imposing fluffy dog breeds, with a thick, heavy double coat that creates a lion-like mane around the neck and shoulders. They typically shed their undercoat once a year in a substantial blow-out, leaving the rest of the year relatively low-shedding.

Tibetan Mastiffs are fiercely independent, highly intelligent, and naturally protective. They're not beginner dogs — they require experienced, confident owners who understand primitive, guardian-type breeds. They make their own decisions, are nocturnal by nature (stemming from their heritage as night-time camp guardians), and can be challenging to train in the traditional sense. With the right owner, they're devoted, noble companions.

Despite the dramatic coat, Tibetan Mastiffs are only moderate maintenance outside shedding season — weekly brushing is generally sufficient. During their annual shed, daily brushing for several weeks is necessary to manage the volume of undercoat. They do not do well in hot, humid climates and are best suited to cooler environments.

### 14. Soft Coated Wheaten Terrier

The Soft Coated Wheaten Terrier is distinctive among terriers for its silky, wavy coat that falls in loose curls and waves — quite different from the wiry coats of most terrier breeds. Their coat is a warm wheaten gold color that lightens as they mature, and it has a silky texture that's wonderfully soft to the touch. Interestingly, Wheatens are considered low-shedding and are sometimes recommended for mild allergy sufferers.

Wheatens are energetic, exuberant, and endlessly entertaining. They retain a strong terrier personality — independent, curious, and playful — but are generally friendlier and more sociable than many other terrier breeds. They're good with children who can match their energy and generally do well with other dogs. They have a characteristic greeting behavior called the "Wheaten Greeting" — a joyful, full-body leap that can knock smaller children over.

Their coat requires regular brushing to prevent mats — 3–4 times per week is recommended. Professional grooming every 6–8 weeks maintains the coat's shape and health. Their coat doesn't shed in the traditional sense; instead, loose hairs tend to mat into the coat and must be brushed out. Regular ear cleaning is also important, as the breed can be prone to ear infections.

### 15. Lhasa Apso

The Lhasa Apso is one of the oldest recognized breeds in the world, developed over 4,000 years ago in Tibetan monasteries as indoor watchdogs for the Dalai Lama. Their long, dense, straight coat parts down the center of the back and falls to the floor in a dramatic curtain, historically meant to provide protection from the harsh mountain climate.

Lhasa Apsos are confident, independent, and possess a keen intelligence. They were bred to make autonomous decisions about who to trust and who to alert against — a quality that makes them suspicious of strangers and less eager to please than many other small breeds. With their family, they're devoted and affectionate, but they expect respect rather than excessive handling. They're assertive and will let you know when they're displeased.

Their coat is extraordinarily high-maintenance when kept long. Daily brushing is essential to prevent matting, and professional grooming every 6–8 weeks is standard. Many owners opt for a practical "puppy cut" that keeps the coat shorter and more manageable. Despite the grooming demands, Lhasa Apsos shed minimally, making the coat more about detangling than dealing with loose hair everywhere.

### 16. Leonberger

The Leonberger was created in the 19th century in Leonberg, Germany, specifically to resemble a lion — and the breed's creator, Heinrich Essig, largely succeeded. These massive dogs have a flowing, medium-long double coat with a spectacular lion-like mane around the neck and chest, particularly pronounced in males. They come in a warm range of golden, red, and mahogany tones.

Leonbergers are gentle, patient, and remarkably family-oriented for a giant breed. They're good with children (though their size alone can cause accidental knockdowns), generally friendly with strangers, and peaceful with other animals. They love water and have a natural affinity for swimming. They're intelligent and trainable, and they thrive on having a meaningful role in family life — they don't do well as purely outdoor dogs.

Their coat sheds heavily — there's no getting around it — and requires brushing 2–3 times per week. The mane is particularly prone to matting and needs daily attention. They blow their undercoat twice a year in dramatic fashion. Despite the grooming demands, Leonbergers are devoted, magnificent companions for families who can accommodate their size and maintenance needs.

### 17. Bearded Collie

The Bearded Collie is a Scottish herding breed with a shaggy, straight double coat that parts naturally down the spine and falls in curtains on either side. Their most distinctive feature is the long hair that frames their face — the "beard" that gives them their name — along with a broad forehead covered in long, sweeping hair. They come in black, blue, brown, or fawn, usually with white markings.

Beardies are enthusiastic, bouncy, and brimming with energy. They were developed to herd in the rugged Scottish Highlands, and they still have that tireless drive today. They need significant daily exercise — multiple walks plus active play — and mental stimulation to prevent boredom. They're intelligent and trainable, though their independent streak means consistent, positive training from an early age is important.

Their coat is notoriously high-maintenance. Without daily brushing, it mats aggressively throughout. The coat also picks up debris from outdoor adventures — leaves, burrs, mud — which must be cleaned out regularly. Professional grooming every 6–8 weeks is standard. Despite these demands, many Beardie owners describe the grooming routine as a bonding experience with their dog.

### 18. Bouvier des Flandres

The Bouvier des Flandres is a powerful Belgian working breed with a thick, rough, shaggy double coat that provides protection against harsh weather. Their tousled appearance belies a serious, capable working dog underneath — they're used as police dogs, search and rescue dogs, and military dogs in several countries. Their coat comes in fawn, black, gray, brindle, or "dirty white" colorings.

Bouviers are confident, intelligent, and intensely loyal. They're not typically lap dogs — they're working animals who bond deeply with their people while maintaining a certain dignified reserve. They require experienced owners who can provide consistent, firm, positive leadership. Without proper training and socialization from an early age, they can become overprotective or difficult to manage.

Their coat requires brushing 2–3 times per week and professional grooming every 6–8 weeks to maintain the proper rough texture. The coat should never be scissored to a smooth finish — it should retain its tousled, natural look. The beard and mustache (like the Schnauzer, Bouviers have facial hair) need regular cleaning to prevent food and bacteria buildup.

### 19. Standard Poodle

The Standard Poodle is the largest of the three Poodle varieties (Toy and Miniature being the others) and is arguably the most elegant of all fluffy dog breeds. Despite their fancy haircuts at dog shows, Poodles were originally developed as water retrievers — their curly, dense coat protected them from cold water. The distinctive clips seen in show rings were originally functional, designed to protect joints and vital organs while allowing freedom of movement in water.

Standard Poodles are highly intelligent — consistently ranked among the top two or three most intelligent dog breeds — and are athletic, trainable, and versatile. They compete in agility, obedience, tracking, and hunting trials. They're affectionate and social, great with families, and their low-shedding coat makes them a practical choice for mild allergy sufferers. Don't let the glamorous appearance fool you — these are active, capable working dogs.

Their curly coat grows continuously and must be clipped every 4–6 weeks to prevent matting. This is a significant ongoing grooming expense. The coat never sheds in the traditional sense; instead, loose hairs curl back into the coat and must be regularly combed out and clipped. Regular ear cleaning is essential due to hair growth within the ear canal.

### 20. Briard

The Briard is an ancient French herding and war dog with one of the most spectacular coats in the canine world. Their long, wavy, coarse outer coat flows in natural parts, completely covering their body and obscuring their eyes behind a thick curtain of hair. They carry a double dewclaw on each rear leg — a breed requirement — and move with a distinctive, gliding, "bear-like" gait. The Briard was used as the official dog of the French Army during World War I.

Briards are loyal, protective, and deeply devoted to their family. They bond intensely with their people and can be suspicious of strangers. They have strong herding instincts and may try to herd children or other animals. They're highly intelligent but can be stubborn, requiring consistent, patient training from an experienced owner. Once trained and socialized, they're magnificent companions.

Their coat is extraordinarily demanding. Daily brushing is required to prevent mats — the coat is long enough to mat at the skin level, which can be painful and lead to skin infections if undetected. Many owners maintain a shorter "working trim" for practicality. Professional grooming every 6–8 weeks is standard. Despite the immense grooming requirements, Briard owners describe their dogs with an almost evangelical devotion — this breed tends to inspire intense loyalty.

## FAQ: Fluffy Dog Breeds

### Do all fluffy dogs shed a lot?

Most fluffy dogs do shed significantly, particularly those with thick double coats. However, a few fluffy breeds are considered low-shedding — the Bichon Frise and Standard Poodle, for example, have curly coats that trap loose hairs rather than releasing them. The Soft Coated Wheaten Terrier and Lhasa Apso also shed less than most fluffy breeds. That said, the vast majority of fluffy dogs on this list shed moderately to heavily, particularly during seasonal coat changes.

### What fluffy dog is best for families with young children?

The Bernese Mountain Dog, Newfoundland, Leonberger, and Old English Sheepdog are famously gentle with children and have the patience and steady temperament that families need. Among smaller fluffy breeds, the Bichon Frise, Pomeranian, and American Eskimo Dog are also good family dogs. Regardless of breed, all dogs should be supervised around young children and all children should be taught how to interact with dogs respectfully.

### How do I manage shedding from a fluffy dog?

Consistent brushing is the single most effective strategy — removing loose fur before it falls dramatically reduces what ends up on your floors and furniture. A high-quality deshedding tool like the FURminator reaches through the topcoat to remove undercoat fur. Regular bathing during shedding season loosens undercoat and speeds the process. A good vacuum designed for pet hair, and furniture covers during heavy shedding periods, also help significantly.

### Are fluffy dogs suitable for hot climates?

Most fluffy dogs with thick double coats were developed for cold climates and struggle in heat. Breeds like the Samoyed, Great Pyrenees, Newfoundland, and Tibetan Mastiff are particularly vulnerable to overheating. If you live in a warm climate and want a fluffy dog, choose a breed that can adapt — like the Standard Poodle or Soft Coated Wheaten Terrier — and always provide air conditioning, shade, and fresh water. Never shave a double-coated dog to keep them cool; their coat actually provides insulation against heat as well as cold, and shaving can damage the coat permanently.

### What's the most low-maintenance fluffy dog?

Relative to the other breeds on this list, the Tibetan Mastiff requires less frequent brushing due to its single annual shed cycle. The Great Pyrenees coat, while voluminous, is naturally resistant to matting and dirt. The Eurasier is also considered relatively moderate-maintenance for a fluffy breed. However, "low-maintenance" is relative — any of the breeds on this list requires significantly more grooming effort than a short-coated breed.
