If you've ever worn black pants around a Golden Retriever, you already understand the fur problem. But here's the good news: not every dog sheds the same way, and several small breeds barely shed at all. Whether you're dealing with allergies, simply tired of lint-rolling every surface in your home, or you're a first-time dog owner wanting a manageable coat, small dogs that don't shed can genuinely change your relationship with pet ownership.

But let's set expectations straight from the start. No dog is completely non-shedding — every dog loses some hair, just like humans do. What makes certain breeds dramatically different is the *rate* and *amount* of shedding, as well as the type of coat they carry. The 15 breeds in this list are among the lowest-shedding small dogs you can own, and each has been recognized by veterinarians and breed experts as a reliable choice for people who want to minimize loose fur in their lives.

## Why Some Dogs Shed Less Than Others

The science behind low-shedding dogs comes down to two main factors: coat type and hair growth cycle.

Most heavy-shedding breeds have what's called a **double coat** — a dense, insulating undercoat beneath a coarser topcoat. The undercoat "blows" seasonally, releasing massive amounts of fur in the spring and fall. Breeds like the Husky, German Shepherd, and Labrador Retriever fall into this category.

Low-shedding breeds typically have one of three coat types:

1. **Single-layer hair coats** — These dogs grow hair rather than fur. Hair has a longer growth cycle before it falls out, meaning it spends more time growing and less time floating around your home. Poodles and Yorkshire Terriers fall into this category.
2. **Continuously growing coats** — Some breeds have hair that grows much like human hair and rarely reaches a natural shedding point without being cut. These dogs need regular grooming but leave minimal hair behind.
3. **Minimal undercoat** — Some single-coated breeds have little to no undercoat, dramatically reducing the seasonal blowout that makes some dogs such prolific shedders.

The distinction between "hair" and "fur" is real but often overstated — both are made of keratin. What matters most is the growth cycle and coat density. Dogs with slower hair growth cycles shed far less visibly.

## The 15 Best Small Dogs That Don't Shed

### 1. Maltese

The Maltese is perhaps the most recognizable low-shedding small dog, and for good reason. These elegant little dogs sport a long, flowing white coat that grows continuously — much like human hair — and drops very little on its own. The coat is a single layer with no undercoat, which is precisely why Maltese owners can sit on a dark sofa without reaching for the lint roller.

Despite the glamorous appearance, Maltese are surprisingly adaptable dogs. They thrive in apartments, do well with seniors, and form incredibly strong bonds with their primary caregiver. They don't require a yard, and their small size makes them easy to transport. The trade-off is that their long coat requires daily brushing to prevent mats and tangles, and regular professional grooming every 6–8 weeks is strongly recommended.

Health-wise, Maltese are generally robust for a toy breed, though they can be prone to dental issues and luxating patellas (knee problems common in small breeds). Their coat, while low-shedding, does require tear stain management around the eyes. Overall, the Maltese is a top pick for allergy sufferers and anyone who prioritizes a tidy home.

### 2. Shih Tzu

The Shih Tzu's name translates from Chinese as "lion dog," and this ancient breed certainly carries itself with regal confidence. Though the Shih Tzu technically has a double coat, both layers are composed of hair (not fur), resulting in very low seasonal shedding. Their coat grows long and luxurious, and because the growth cycle is so extended, hair rarely falls out naturally — it mostly stays put until it's brushed or trimmed.

Shih Tzus are famously affectionate and people-oriented. They were bred as companion dogs for Chinese royalty and have never lost that lap-dog mentality. They're low-energy, happy to spend hours cuddled on the couch, and generally get along well with children and other pets. They're not known for excessive barking, which makes them excellent apartment dogs.

Grooming a Shih Tzu does require commitment. Their coat mats easily if not brushed every day or two, and many owners opt for a "puppy cut" — a shorter, uniform trim — to make maintenance more manageable. Regular ear cleaning and eye care are also essential parts of their routine.

### 3. Toy Poodle

The Toy Poodle stands out as one of the best all-around dogs for allergy sufferers. Their distinctive curly coat is a single layer of tightly wound hair that traps shed hairs within the curl rather than releasing them into the air or onto your furniture. This means they're among the closest thing to a truly hypoallergenic dog you'll find.

Beyond the hypoallergenic coat, Toy Poodles are extraordinarily intelligent — consistently ranked among the smartest dog breeds in the world. They learn commands quickly, excel at tricks, and thrive with mental stimulation. They're energetic for their size, playful, and social, making them excellent companions for active individuals or families with children.

The grooming requirement for Toy Poodles is significant. Their coat grows continuously and must be trimmed every 4–6 weeks to prevent matting. Many owners learn to groom at home, which reduces costs over time. Their ears require regular cleaning to prevent infections, as the curly hair around ear canals can trap moisture.

### 4. Yorkshire Terrier

The Yorkshire Terrier — affectionately known as the Yorkie — is a tiny dog with a huge personality and a coat that behaves more like human hair than dog fur. Yorkies have a single-layer, fine, silky coat with no undercoat, making them one of the lowest-shedding breeds in existence. Their hair grows long and falls in a straight, glossy curtain when kept at full length.

Yorkies are spirited, bold, and surprisingly feisty for their size. They were originally bred to hunt rats in textile mills in Yorkshire, England, and they retain that tenacious, curious personality today. They love their people deeply and can be protective, sometimes to the point of bossiness with larger dogs. Early socialization helps temper this tendency.

One important grooming note: while Yorkies barely shed, their long coats require daily brushing and trimming every 6–8 weeks. Many owners keep their Yorkies in a "puppy cut" for easier maintenance. Their facial hair can irritate their eyes if left too long, so regular trimming around the face is important.

### 5. Bichon Frise

The Bichon Frise is a cheerful, cloud-like small dog known for its white, powder-puff coat and near-total absence of shedding. Like the Poodle, the Bichon's curly coat traps loose hairs internally rather than depositing them on furniture. This makes the Bichon one of the most consistently recommended breeds for allergy sufferers.

Bichons are gentle, playful, and adaptable. They're excellent with children, get along with other pets, and are known for their happy-go-lucky disposition. They love attention and don't do well with long periods of isolation — if you work from home or have flexible hours, the Bichon Frise will be your devoted shadow and constant source of entertainment.

Grooming a Bichon requires regular brushing to prevent mats, and professional trimming every 4–6 weeks keeps that signature rounded silhouette. They're prone to skin allergies and tear staining, so dietary choices and regular facial grooming are worth paying attention to. Overall, the Bichon Frise is an outstanding choice for families, seniors, and anyone seeking a small, low-shedding companion.

### 6. Miniature Schnauzer

The Miniature Schnauzer is the smallest of the three Schnauzer sizes and the most popular Schnauzer variety in the United States. They have a distinctive wiry double coat — a harder outer layer with a soft undercoat — but unlike many double-coated breeds, they shed very minimally. The wiry texture of the coat means loose hairs tend to stay entangled in the topcoat rather than floating free.

Mini Schnauzers are highly intelligent, energetic, and affectionate. They were bred as working farm dogs in Germany and still carry that alert, curious personality. They're known for their boldness and can be vocal, making them surprisingly effective watchdogs despite their small size. They do well with active families and generally enjoy having a job to do, whether that's a daily walk, agility training, or interactive puzzle toys.

The coat requires hand-stripping or regular clipping every 5–8 weeks to maintain its wiry texture and low-shedding properties. Without regular grooming, the coat can soften over time, which while not harmful, changes the texture and may slightly increase shedding. Their distinctive beard and eyebrows should be cleaned regularly to prevent food build-up and skin irritation.

### 7. Havanese

The Havanese is the national dog of Cuba and one of the most versatile and charming small breeds available. Their silky, long coat is naturally low-shedding — it's a single layer of soft hair that grows slowly and doesn't undergo the dramatic seasonal blowouts common in double-coated breeds. Havanese come in a wide array of colors and can be kept in various coat lengths depending on owner preference.

Havanese dogs are social, happy, and trainable. They were bred exclusively as companion dogs for Cuban aristocracy, and their entire purpose in life is to be with their people. They adapt well to different living situations, get along with children and other animals, and are known for their clownish, entertaining personalities. They can be trained to perform tricks easily and enjoy learning new things.

Grooming is a daily commitment for Havanese kept in a long coat — brushing is essential to prevent mats. Many owners opt for a shorter trim, sometimes called a "teddy bear cut," which significantly reduces grooming time while preserving the breed's adorable appearance. Their drop ears should be checked and cleaned regularly.

### 8. Bolognese

The Bolognese is a lesser-known gem among low-shedding small dogs. Originating in Bologna, Italy, these dogs have a distinctive fluffy, non-curling white coat that resembles the Bichon Frise but has a unique flocked texture. They shed extremely little and are considered a good option for mild allergy sufferers.

Bolognese dogs are devoted and calm, forming extremely close bonds with their owners. They tend to be quieter and more reserved than some other companion breeds, and they can be somewhat shy around strangers. Early socialization is important to help them develop confidence. They're not as energetic as Poodles or Schnauzers — a daily walk and some indoor play is generally sufficient exercise.

Their coat requires regular brushing to prevent tangling, though it mats less aggressively than some other long-coated breeds. Professional grooming every 6–8 weeks is recommended. The Bolognese remains relatively rare outside of Europe, so finding a reputable breeder may require some research. They tend to be long-lived and healthy dogs overall.

### 9. Chinese Crested

The Chinese Crested is one of the most unique-looking dogs in existence, and arguably the most hypoallergenic of all small breeds. The Hairless variety has bare skin over most of the body with only tufts of hair on the head (the "crest"), feet ("socks"), and tail ("plume"), meaning virtually no shedding occurs. The Powderpuff variety has a full silky coat but still sheds minimally.

Despite their unusual appearance, Chinese Cresteds are affectionate, playful, and deeply attached to their owners. They're often described as cat-like in their behavior — agile, curious, and fond of perching in high places. They're excellent for small spaces and do well with seniors and less active owners. The hairless variety is especially suited to allergy sufferers.

The bare skin of the Hairless variety requires specific care: sunscreen on sunny days, moisturizer to prevent dryness, and protection from cold temperatures. Their exposed skin is prone to acne and sunburn. The Powderpuff needs regular brushing and occasional trimming. Both varieties need routine dental care, as tooth problems are common in the breed.

### 10. Coton de Tulear

The Coton de Tulear — named for its cotton-like coat and the city of Tulear in Madagascar — is a rare and delightful breed that's gaining popularity for its low-shedding coat and wonderful temperament. Their coat is soft, fluffy, and slightly wavy, and they shed very little throughout the year. Cotons are classified as hypoallergenic by many allergy organizations.

Cotons are cheerful, adaptable, and extremely social. They love people, get along beautifully with children and other pets, and tend to be easy-going about new environments. They're not overly demanding in terms of exercise — they enjoy walks and playtime but are equally happy relaxing with their family. Their gentle nature makes them excellent therapy dogs.

Grooming a Coton requires daily brushing to keep the cottony coat from matting, particularly behind the ears and in the armpits. Professional grooming every 6–8 weeks helps manage the coat length. They're generally healthy dogs with fewer breed-specific health concerns than many other toy breeds, which is another point in their favor.

### 11. Affenpinscher

The Affenpinscher — German for "monkey-like terrier" — is a small but spunky dog with a rough, wiry coat that sheds minimally. Their dense, harsh fur has a texture that tends to hold loose hairs in place rather than releasing them into your environment. They're not often listed among the top hypoallergenic breeds, but they're genuinely low-maintenance from a shedding standpoint.

Affenpinschers are confident, curious, and entertaining. They have a legendary stubbornness that makes training both challenging and rewarding — they respond best to positive reinforcement and short, engaging training sessions. They can be feisty with other dogs and should be socialized early, but with their families they're loyal and genuinely funny companions. They love to play and can be surprisingly energetic for their size.

Their wiry coat requires hand-stripping or regular trimming to maintain proper texture, typically every few months. Brushing a few times per week is usually sufficient between professional grooming sessions. The Affenpinscher is a good match for experienced dog owners who appreciate a dog with an independent streak and a big personality packed into a tiny body.

### 12. Brussels Griffon

The Brussels Griffon comes in two coat types — rough and smooth — but both are considered low-shedding relative to many other breeds. The rough-coated variety has a wiry, dense coat similar to the Affenpinscher, while the smooth variety has a short, glossy coat. Neither variety is a heavy shedder, and the rough variety especially retains loose hairs within the coat.

Brussels Griffons are loyal, sensitive, and surprisingly expressive — their flat, human-like faces give them an almost cartoonish emotional range. They bond intensely with one person but are generally friendly and curious. They can be somewhat sensitive to rough handling, making them better suited to homes with older, gentler children. They're intelligent but can be strong-willed during training.

### 13. Japanese Chin

The Japanese Chin is an ancient breed with aristocratic origins in the Japanese imperial court. Despite having a flowing, silky coat, the Japanese Chin sheds surprisingly little. Their coat is a single layer that lacks the dense undercoat responsible for the heavy shedding in many other breeds. They have a self-cleaning quality to their coat, somewhat like a cat.

Japanese Chins are calm, dignified, and affectionate. They're known for their cat-like behaviors — they perch on high surfaces, use their paws to wash their faces, and tend to be somewhat quiet and reserved. They're well-suited to apartment living and don't require significant exercise. They're sensitive to extreme temperatures, particularly heat, due to their flat faces.

Their coat is relatively easy to care for given its length and silkiness — brushing two or three times per week is usually sufficient, and they typically don't need professional grooming as frequently as other long-haired breeds. Their facial wrinkles should be cleaned regularly to prevent skin fold dermatitis.

### 14. Toy Fox Terrier

The Toy Fox Terrier may surprise some readers on this list — short-coated dogs aren't always associated with low shedding. But the Toy Fox Terrier's coat is so short and fine that visible shedding is minimal. What little they do shed is barely noticeable, and they require almost no grooming beyond occasional baths and wipe-downs.

Toy Fox Terriers are athletic, energetic, and highly intelligent. They were bred from the Smooth Fox Terrier and several toy breeds, inheriting sharp instincts and a tireless drive to play. They excel at agility, are easy to train with positive reinforcement, and form strong bonds with active owners. They may not be the best match for households with very young children, as they can be nippy if mishandled.

Their grooming needs are about as low as it gets — a soft brush and occasional bath is all they need. Their short coats offer no protection in cold weather, so a dog coat or sweater is recommended in winter months. The Toy Fox Terrier is an excellent low-maintenance choice for active owners who want a spirited, energetic companion without the shedding.

### 15. Miniature Pinscher

The Miniature Pinscher, often called the "Min Pin," is a sleek, athletic breed with a short, smooth coat that sheds very little. They're sometimes mistaken for miniature Dobermans, but they're actually an older and entirely separate breed. Their tight, hard coat is easy to maintain and produces minimal shedding throughout the year.

Min Pins are bold, energetic, and fearlessly confident — they're sometimes called the "King of Toys" for their proud, prancing gait and self-assured personality. They require active owners who can provide adequate exercise and mental stimulation. They can be escape artists, so secure fencing is important. With proper socialization, they're loyal and entertaining companions.

Like the Toy Fox Terrier, grooming a Min Pin is simple: a quick brush-down with a soft bristle brush once or twice a week is all it takes. They're sensitive to cold, so protective clothing in colder climates is helpful. Their short coat also means they're easier to check for skin issues, ticks, and external parasites.

## FAQ: Small Dogs That Don't Shed

### Do small dogs shed less than large dogs?

Not necessarily — size alone doesn't determine how much a dog sheds. A small Beagle can shed far more than a large Standard Poodle. Shedding is primarily determined by coat type, the presence or absence of an undercoat, and the hair growth cycle. Small breeds like Maltese and Yorkshire Terriers shed minimally because of their coat structure, not their size.

### Are small hypoallergenic dogs truly allergy-safe?

The term "hypoallergenic" is somewhat misleading. No dog is 100% allergy-free, because human dog allergies are typically triggered by a protein called Can f 1 found in dog saliva, dander, and urine — not just hair. However, low-shedding dogs spread less dander and allergen-coated hair around the home, which does reduce allergic reactions for many people. If you have severe allergies, spend time with the specific breed before committing.

### What's the easiest small dog to groom?

From a grooming standpoint, the Chinese Crested (Hairless variety), Miniature Pinscher, and Toy Fox Terrier require the least grooming effort. Breeds with long, silky, or curly coats — like the Poodle, Maltese, or Havanese — require more frequent brushing and professional grooming, though they still shed minimally.

### Can low-shedding dogs still cause allergies?

Yes. As mentioned above, the primary dog allergen is a protein in saliva and skin cells, not hair itself. Low-shedding dogs produce fewer airborne allergens because they're not depositing hair (and the dander it carries) around the home, but they can still cause allergic reactions. Regular bathing of the dog and keeping them off furniture can help reduce allergen levels in the home.

### How often should I groom a low-shedding small dog?

It depends on the breed. Dogs with long or curly coats (Poodle, Bichon, Maltese) need brushing every 1–2 days and professional grooming every 4–8 weeks. Dogs with short, smooth coats (Miniature Pinscher, Toy Fox Terrier) only need brushing once or twice a week and occasional baths. Wiry-coated breeds (Miniature Schnauzer, Affenpinscher) fall somewhere in between and benefit from hand-stripping or clipping a few times per year.

### Which small non-shedding dog is best for first-time owners?

The Bichon Frise, Havanese, and Maltese are consistently recommended for first-time dog owners. They have gentle, trainable personalities, adapt well to different living situations, and are forgiving of beginner mistakes. The Toy Poodle is also an excellent choice if the owner is prepared to provide adequate mental stimulation and exercise.
