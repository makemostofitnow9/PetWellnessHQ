Your dog just vomited three times and won't touch their food. Or they have diarrhea and you're doing laundry you didn't plan on doing. Whatever the situation, an upset stomach in a dog is stressful — for the dog and the owner.

Most cases of acute GI upset in dogs are self-limiting, meaning they resolve on their own within 24–48 hours. The right food choices during this window can significantly speed recovery. Here's exactly what to do.

## Step One: When to Fast (and When Not To)

For a healthy adult dog with mild vomiting or diarrhea and no other symptoms, a short fast of **8–12 hours** (not overnight for puppies — they need calories) allows the GI tract to settle.

**Do NOT fast your dog if they:**
- Are under 6 months old
- Are diabetic
- Are pregnant or nursing
- Have a history of hypoglycemia
- Seem lethargic or weak
- Have blood in vomit or stool
- Are vomiting repeatedly with no relief

In any of these cases, call your vet before withholding food.

## The Bland Diet: The Standard Recovery Protocol

After a short fast (or skipping the fast for puppies and vulnerable dogs), the go-to is a **bland diet** — easily digestible food that's gentle on an irritated GI tract.

### Option 1: Plain Boiled Chicken + White Rice

The classic. Boneless skinless chicken breast, boiled with no seasoning, shredded fine. Combined with plain white rice (not brown — it's higher in fiber, which you don't want right now) in a ratio of roughly:
- **1 part chicken : 2 parts rice**

Start with small portions — about **1/3 of your dog's normal meal size** — and feed 3–4 small meals instead of 1–2 large ones.

### Option 2: Plain Boiled Turkey + White Rice

Same principle, just turkey instead of chicken. Some dogs do better with turkey. Same rules: no seasoning, no skin, no bones.

### Option 3: Cooked Plain White Rice Alone

For the first meal after a fast, some dogs do best with just plain rice. It's bland, starchy, and easy to digest.

### Option 4: Plain Pumpkin Purée

Canned pumpkin (pure pumpkin — **not** pumpkin pie filling, which contains sugar and spices) is a vet-recommended tool for both diarrhea and constipation because of its soluble fiber content.

**Dose:** 1 teaspoon for small dogs, 1 tablespoon for medium dogs, 2 tablespoons for large dogs, added to food.

### Option 5: Prescription GI Food

If you have a bag of Hills I/D, Royal Canin Gastrointestinal, or Purina EN on hand from a previous vet visit, these are excellent options. They're formulated specifically for GI recovery.

## Foods to Avoid During GI Upset

Keep these away from your dog until they're fully recovered:
- Regular kibble (temporarily too hard to digest)
- Fatty foods of any kind
- Dairy
- High-fiber foods (sweet potato, veggies, brown rice)
- Any human food with seasoning, onion, garlic, or spices
- Treats

## Hydration: Often the Most Important Factor

Diarrhea and vomiting cause fluid loss, and dehydration is the main risk of GI upset. Make sure your dog has constant access to fresh water.

**Signs of dehydration:**
- Gums that are tacky rather than moist
- Skin that doesn't spring back when gently pinched
- Sunken eyes
- Lethargy

If you're concerned about dehydration, offering small amounts of unflavored Pedialyte (1 part Pedialyte : 1 part water) can help replace electrolytes. Call your vet if your dog won't drink.

## Transitioning Back to Normal Food

Don't switch back to regular food abruptly. Follow this schedule:

| Day | Diet |
|-----|------|
| Day 1 | Bland diet only (small, frequent meals) |
| Day 2 | Bland diet if symptoms have resolved |
| Day 3 | 75% bland / 25% regular food |
| Day 4 | 50% bland / 50% regular food |
| Day 5 | 25% bland / 75% regular food |
| Day 6 | Back to normal food |

If symptoms return at any transition point, go back a step and give it another day.

## Helpful Supplements

### Probiotics
Probiotic supplements designed for dogs can help restore healthy gut flora after GI upset, especially if antibiotics are involved. Options include:
- FortiFlora (Purina — the most vet-recommended)
- Proviable
- Native Pet Probiotic

### Plain Yogurt (Sparingly)
A small spoonful of plain, unsweetened yogurt with live cultures can provide beneficial bacteria. Not all dogs tolerate dairy, so introduce carefully.

## When to Call the Vet

Mild GI upset is common and usually nothing to worry about. But some symptoms require prompt veterinary attention.

**Call your vet if your dog:**
- Vomits more than 3 times in 24 hours
- Has diarrhea for more than 48 hours
- Shows blood in vomit or stool (red blood or dark, tarry stool)
- Is lethargic or unresponsive
- Has a distended or painful abdomen
- Ate something they shouldn't have (especially toys, bones, medications, plants, or chemicals)
- Is a puppy, senior, or has underlying health conditions
- Shows signs of dehydration

**Call immediately (emergency) if:**
- Your dog is trying to vomit repeatedly but nothing is coming up — this is a sign of bloat (GDV), which is life-threatening
- They collapse or are extremely weak
- You know they ingested a toxin

## Common Causes of GI Upset in Dogs

Understanding the cause helps prevent recurrence:
- **Dietary indiscretion** — ate something they shouldn't (most common cause)
- **Sudden food change** — switching foods too quickly
- **Table scraps or fatty foods** — especially rich foods, which can trigger pancreatitis
- **Stress** — travel, boarding, new pets, changes in routine
- **Viral or bacterial infection** — parvovirus, salmonella
- **Parasites** — giardia, roundworms, hookworms
- **Medication side effects** — especially antibiotics and NSAIDs
- **Inflammatory bowel disease** — in dogs with recurrent issues

## The Bottom Line

A single episode of vomiting or a day of loose stools usually isn't cause for alarm. Fast briefly, introduce bland food slowly, keep your dog hydrated, and watch carefully. Most dogs bounce back within 24–48 hours.

What matters most is knowing when it's more than just a stomach bug. When in doubt, call your vet — a quick phone consultation is almost always available and will tell you whether you need to come in.
